The step-by-step

Eight steps from "I want to bid on federal contracts" to "I'm in the system." The waiting is the hard part — the work itself is straightforward if you prepare carefully.

1

Gather your information before you start

Most registration delays come from missing or mismatched information. Get all of this ready in one place before you touch sam.gov:

  • Your legal business name, exactly as it appears on your IRS records — punctuation, abbreviations, capitalization, all of it
  • Your EIN (Employer Identification Number) and the legal name attached to it
  • Your physical business address — no P.O. boxes
  • Your banking information for federal payment by EFT (routing and account numbers)
  • Your NAICS codes — the industry classifications for your business
  • Names and emails for three designated contacts at your company. Federal agencies will reach these people about your contracts. The roles are: Government Business (contracting officer's main contact), Electronic Business (federal payments), and Past Performance (agency evaluation surveys). The same person can hold all three roles.
Pro tip: The single most common rejection reason is a mismatch between the business name you enter and the name the IRS has on file. Pull out your IRS EIN confirmation letter (CP 575) and copy the name character for character. If you can't find the letter, call the IRS Business and Specialty Tax Line at 800-829-4933 to confirm.
2

Create a Login.gov account

Login.gov is the federal government's universal sign-in service. You'll need an account to access SAM.gov.

Create your Login.gov account →

Use a permanent business email — not a personal Gmail you might stop checking. Federal correspondence about your registration goes here, and a missed email can stall your application by weeks.
3

Begin entity registration on SAM.gov

Go to the official site — only ever sam.gov. Beware of look-alike domains charging fees. Registration is always free.

From the home page, click Get Started under Entity Registration. You'll choose your entity type (most people select Business or Organization) and enter your legal name and physical address.

Start your SAM.gov registration →

4

Validate your entity

SAM.gov cross-checks your business name and address against IRS and commercial databases. If they match, the system displays your UEI (Unique Entity Identifier) — a 12-character ID that replaced the old DUNS number.

If validation fails, you'll be asked to upload supporting documentation (formation document, IRS letter, etc.) to prove your business exists as you described it.

This step accounts for the longest delays. Name mismatches add days or weeks. Take time to verify your information before submitting.
5

Complete the registration form

The full form has about 15 sections covering business info, financial info, NAICS codes, certifications, and representations. Plan for one to three hours of focused work, ideally split across two sessions.

The most consequential sections:

  • NAICS codes — pick your primary plus any secondary codes that genuinely describe your work. These determine which opportunities you'll be eligible for.
  • Banking information — federal payments are EFT-only. Errors here delay your future payments.
  • Representations & Certifications — these are legal attestations. Read every one carefully. False statements carry serious penalties.
6

Get your CAGE code

CAGE (Commercial and Government Entity) codes are assigned by the Defense Logistics Agency. If you don't already have one, SAM.gov requests it on your behalf during registration.

CAGE assignment usually takes 3–5 business days on top of the SAM processing time. If it's been over 10 business days, contact DLA at cagemail@dla.mil or 1-877-352-2255.
7

Submit and wait

After you submit, several validations run in parallel: IRS TIN matching, CAGE code assignment, and entity verification.

Expected timeline:

  • Official: 7–10 business days
  • Realistic average: 2–4 weeks
  • If issues arise: 6–10 weeks (validation failures, name mismatches)

Watch your email closely. SAM.gov will send messages if anything needs correction — a slow reply on your end becomes a multi-week delay.

Pitfalls that delay registrations

Drawn from thousands of real registration outcomes — these are the failure patterns that turn a 10-day process into a 6-week one.

Legal name doesn't match IRS records
Smith & Associates LLC is not the same as Smith and Associates, LLC to the validation system. Punctuation, abbreviations, and capitalization all matter.
Invalid or outdated point-of-contact email
If a POC email belongs to someone no longer with your company, the registration is rejected. Use stable business addresses, not personal ones.
EIN/TIN mismatch with IRS
The legal name attached to your EIN must match exactly. Recent name changes that haven't been reflected in IRS records will fail validation.
Missing notarized administrator letter
New entities are required to submit a notarized letter appointing an Entity Administrator. Get it notarized early — local notary availability adds days.
Letting your registration expire
Registrations expire annually. There is no grace period — the moment they expire, you are inactive in SAM.gov searches and ineligible for new awards.
